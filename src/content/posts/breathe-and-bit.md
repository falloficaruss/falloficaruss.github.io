## Where the Island Breathes Twice

On Gili Iyang, time does not move in a straight line; it gathers and releases, like breath taken in before prayer. To arrive here is to enter a place where landscape and ritual still speak to one another. The island is often described through its air, but what matters more than any measurable atmosphere is the way breathing itself becomes a social and spiritual idiom: a way of marking presence, memory, and relation.

As dawn breaks, the light arrives not with a crash, but with a slow, bruised violet creeping over the Java Sea. The salt-mist hangs heavy, a translucent shroud that blurs the distinction between the limestone cliffs and the waking sky. At this hour, the participants of the *Andherenat* ritual begin to gather. There is no heraldic trumpet, no formal call to order. Instead, there is a gradual coalescence of shadows. Men and women emerge from the canopy of coconut palms, their footsteps muffled by the damp earth. 

The soundscape is a palimpsest of Islamic devotion and local inheritance. There is *tawassul*, the seeking of intercession; there is the recitation of *yasin*; there is *tahlil*, the repetition of *la ilaha illa Allah* as a sonic anchor that gathers separate bodies into a shared pulse. And there is the *topak lober* procession, whose movement through space makes the village itself part of the rite. None of this fits neatly into the false choice between "orthodox religion" and "local custom." The ritual lives precisely in their entanglement.



Watch them as they prepare. They are stepping out of ordinary time and into ritual time. In their daily lives, these people are farmers, fishers, laborers, migrants, parents, and parishioners of the state. They carry identity cards, debts, obligations, and ambitions. But as the ritual begins, those identities start to fray at the edges.

The island itself seems to circle inward. Geographically, it is a speck of coral and stone; spiritually, it is a centrifuge. The *Andherenat* is a closing of the circle, a way for the island to recognize itself before the sun climbs too high. But a question hangs in the humid air, unvoiced but heavy: What does it mean to belong to a place when the place itself is shifting beneath your feet? When the ritual is no longer just a conversation with ancestors, but a negotiation with modernity, tourism, and the encroaching reach of a state that demands legibility?

To understand the *Andherenat* is to understand a broader human problem: how communities preserve spaces of transformation in a world that increasingly prefers stability, classification, and administrative clarity.

## The Threshold Where Names Dissolve

To enter the ritual is to step onto the *limen*—the threshold. Arnold van Gennep gave us the classic three-part structure of rites of passage: separation, liminality, incorporation. Victor Turner later deepened the middle term in [*The Ritual Process*](https://www.routledge.com/The-Ritual-Process-Structure-and-Anti-Structure/Turner-Abrahams-Harris/p/book/9781315134666), describing liminality as the "betwixt and between" state in which ordinary classifications lose their hold. On Gili Iyang, this is not a dry academic concept; it is a felt condition of social and spiritual suspension.

Imagine the shedding of a skin. As the *Andherenat* progresses, the social markers that define the villagers begin to loosen. The wealthy landowner and the landless laborer sit shoulder to shoulder in the dust. The hierarchy of the street gives way, however briefly, to what Turner called *communitas*: a mode of relation grounded less in rank than in shared exposure to the threshold. Titles, debts, and grievances do not vanish forever, but they are temporarily displaced by participation in a common rite.



### The Mechanics of the Void
Liminality is often misunderstood as a simple waiting room. In reality, it is a charged interval in which structures are unsettled without yet being replaced. In Turner’s terms, liminal persons can become "structurally invisible": difficult to place within the settled grid of everyday life. On the island, the choreography of the ritual, the repetition of chant, and the movement of the procession together loosen the hold of ordinary identity.

The philosophical weight of this cannot be overstated. If identity is a house we live in, liminality is the moment its walls become permeable. For a duration, one stands exposed to relation, memory, and uncertainty. The threshold cannot be mapped like territory because it exists in passage, not possession.

### The Modern Hunger for the Liminal
Why does this ancient island ritual resonate so deeply with the "civilized" observer? Perhaps because modern life has become a desert of the liminal. We have replaced rites of passage with bureaucratic milestones. We graduate, we get promoted, we retire—but these are legalistic transitions, not spiritual ones. They lack the "risk" of the threshold. 

In a neoliberal world, we are told that identity is a product we can purchase or a brand we can curate. We are encouraged to be "authentic," but only within the predictable confines of a market-friendly persona. We are never truly allowed to be *nothing*. We are never permitted to enter the silence of the *Andherenat*, where names dissolve and the self becomes a fluid, unmapped territory.



As the ritual on Gili Iyang reaches its zenith, the participants are not merely remembering a past; they are remaking a present. Identity appears not as a fixed inheritance but as something repeatedly assembled, loosened, and reassembled in communal life.

However, this fluidity is a threat. To the state, to the institution, and to the market, a person who can dissolve their name is a person who cannot be easily governed. And so, the threshold is always under siege.

## The Third Shore That Cannot Be Mapped

When the ritual ends and the participants step back into the light of the midday sun, they do not simply return to who they were before. They carry the threshold back with them. This is where hybridity becomes useful as a way of thinking.

Homi Bhabha’s notion of the "Third Space" helps name what is at stake here. Hybridity is not mere mixture, nor is it evidence of impurity or loss. It is the production of a space in which inherited categories can no longer remain sovereign. The *Andherenat* is not reducible to "Islam" on one side and "tradition" on the other. It stages their encounter, overlap, and mutual translation.

This is the "Third Shore" of the essay: a site of invention that refuses the binary of "traditional" versus "modern." The people of Gili Iyang are not relics of a pure past, nor are they simply absorbed by the outside world. They are makers of a hybrid self. Purity, in this frame, appears less as a moral ideal than as a political fiction maintained by institutions that prefer clean boundaries.



If identity is a palimpsest, then the ritual is the moment when several layers of writing become visible at once. We see prayer, ancestry, administration, aspiration, and local memory appearing in the same scene without collapsing into a single meaning.

The tension, of course, is that the institutions that govern our lives insist on clarity. They demand to know: *Are you a citizen or an outsider? Are you orthodox or heterodox? Are you preserving heritage or modernizing?* The ritual answers not by choosing one side, but by inhabiting the unstable interval between them.

## From Ritual to Legibility

If the ritual is a breathing cycle, the modern state is a counting machine. To ground the abstractions of liminality, we have to look at the material architecture of cultural management. James C. Scott’s phrase [*seeing like a state*](https://politicalscience.yale.edu/publications/seeing-state-how-certain-schemes-improve-human-condition-have-failed) remains useful here: before a state can govern, it must render people, practices, and spaces legible. It prefers forms that can be counted, classified, archived, and displayed.

### The Ledger of Legibility
In the Indonesian archipelago, as elsewhere, one of the state’s primary tools is documentary identity. The participant who has just passed through a ritual that loosens social categories must still return to forms that demand stable ones: a singular affiliation, a fixed domicile, a legible household, a recognizable role. The contrast is not absolute, but it is telling. Ritual tolerates ambiguity; administration struggles with it.

The friction appears when a transformative practice must be translated into administrative language. In that translation, the *Andherenat* risks becoming less a lived passage than a cultural object: something to be listed, managed, safeguarded, or leveraged.

### The Extraction of the Intangible
We move now from the identity form to the language of cultural heritage. The [UNESCO Convention for the Safeguarding of the Intangible Cultural Heritage](https://ich.unesco.org/en/convention) offers one influential template for valuing ritual, memory, and performance. Yet safeguarding is never neutral. To preserve a practice institutionally is also to name it, frame it, and sometimes freeze it.

When rituals such as the *Andherenat*, or the village festivals discussed in Malta, are folded into heritage discourse, they can undergo a semiotic shift. A dangerous and transformative threshold may be reframed as a sign of identity, a marker of local distinctiveness, or a contribution to cultural capital. What was once lived as unsettling can be curated as exemplary.

This is where the comparison to neo-colonial governance matters. In the Maltese case, especially under the shadow of the [European Capital of Culture](https://culture.ec.europa.eu/policies/culture-in-cities-and-regions/about-european-capitals-of-culture) and the language of cultural capital, local festivity becomes increasingly entangled with branding, development, and the demand that culture justify itself in economic terms. The same logic can illuminate the pressures facing Gili Iyang: ritual is valued, but on terms set by institutions that want it visible, stable, and useful.


## The Architecture of Managed Hybridity

To bridge this to the state, we have to look at how governance borrows the language of care. Preservation, development, inclusion, and modernization can all sound benevolent. But in post-colonial settings, such vocabularies often carry neo-colonial effects. They make local life answerable to distant institutions, external benchmarks, and administrative visions of what a place ought to become.

### The Politics of Readability
On Gili Iyang, what matters is not only whether the island is measured, but how measurement changes the terms of recognition. Once a place is known through promoted features, administratively useful narratives, or development categories, those descriptions begin to organize what can be said about it and what can be done to it.

The result is not always direct prohibition. More often, it is subtler: local practices are encouraged insofar as they can be rendered presentable, legible, and compatible with official narratives of heritage or development. This is what makes symbolic violence such an apt concept. The community is not simply coerced from outside; it is asked to recognize itself through categories that narrow what it can publicly be.

### The Symbolic Violence of the "Smart Island"
The language of modernization often arrives with a promise of efficiency and inclusion. Yet such frameworks can flatten identity into dashboards, visitor narratives, or predictable forms of participation. What does not fit those forms becomes difficult to see and therefore difficult to defend.

The villagers’ pathways of memory, obligation, and ritual movement do not disappear because a map omits them. But omission matters. The threshold remains real in lived experience while becoming increasingly hard to defend in the public languages of policy, planning, and optimization.

## The Heresy of Clarity: A Counter-Movement

At this point, a pause is necessary. It would be easy to romanticize ritual as pure resistance and condemn the state as pure abstraction. The sources do not permit such simplicity. The threshold is real, but it does not abolish inequality. Hybridity is productive, but it does not dissolve power.

### The Skepticism of the Ledger
The idea of *communitas* should not be mistaken for a permanent social fact. A ritual may suspend hierarchy without erasing it. The landowner and the laborer can kneel in the same circle and still return afterward to very different material worlds.

The skeptic’s question is therefore legitimate: does the ritual dissolve power, or can it also conceal it? Any serious reading has to hold both possibilities in view. Liminality opens a zone of transformation, but that zone exists inside a larger social order that may reassert itself with force once the rite ends.

### The Failure of the "Hybrid" Buzzword
Likewise, hybridity should not become a celebratory buzzword that hides loss. The mixing of registers, languages, and symbols can be a creative practice, but it can also emerge under pressure, especially where education, religion, and administration reward some forms of speech while marginalizing others.

When we celebrate a hybrid form, we should therefore ask a harder question: hybrid on whose terms? Sometimes hybridity is a tactic of resilience; sometimes it is a compromise extracted by unequal conditions. The "Third Space" remains analytically powerful, but only if it is kept tied to history, coercion, and material struggle.


## Synthesis: The Data of the Threshold

We have moved from the violet dawn of ritual into the harder light of governance. What emerges is not a rejection of theory, but a more disciplined use of it:
1.  **Liminality names a real social threshold:** van Gennep and Turner help explain how the *Andherenat* suspends ordinary classifications and generates *communitas*.
2.  **Hybridity is not impurity but negotiation:** Bhabha’s "Third Space" clarifies how Islamic practice and local cosmology coexist without resolving into a single essence.
3.  **Governance seeks legibility:** Scott helps explain why states and institutions prefer rituals as heritage, identity, or development assets rather than as unruly zones of transformation.
4.  **Neo-colonial management works through valuation:** the comparison with Malta and the language of cultural capital shows how local practices can be recognized and contained at the same time.

The central insight is that human identity is contested between lived, liminal experience and the systems that seek to stabilize it. Ritual does not escape power. But neither is it exhausted by the categories through which power describes it.

To live between community and the state is to inhabit a world where one’s deepest forms of belonging remain only partially translatable into official language. The threshold endures, but under pressure.

## The Return That Is Never a Return

To walk back into the world after the *Andherenat* is to enter van Gennep’s final stage: incorporation, the return to communal life. But the return is never a simple restoration. Those who re-enter daily life do so carrying the residue of a threshold that has shown them another way of being together.

The modern tension lies here: participants return from a rite that has loosened names, but they return to institutions that ask them to inhabit those names more firmly than ever. The return is therefore not a restoration of the old self, but a negotiation with systems that demand consistency while life keeps producing ambiguity.

### Why the Threshold Matters
We should recognize what is at stake when liminal spaces narrow. A society that cannot tolerate ambiguity loses one of its ways of processing grief, transition, obligation, and change. When ritual becomes only spectacle, branding, or managed heritage, something vital is diminished: not folklore alone, but a communal technology for living through uncertainty.

The state may welcome the visible appearance of ritual, especially when it can stand for identity, continuity, or development. What is harder to accommodate is ritual’s substance: its capacity to suspend hierarchy, complicate classification, and produce forms of belonging not easily captured by policy.



### To Live Between the Bit and the Breath
Synthesis, then, is not found in a return to a pure past that never existed, nor in surrender to the abstractions of governance. It is found in the continued defense of spaces where people can become otherwise than the categories assigned to them.

The future of human identity may well involve what some theorists call permanent liminality: a condition in which people are repeatedly made to navigate overlapping systems, partial belongings, and unstable boundaries. In such a world, ritual remains important not because it solves instability, but because it gives instability form, meaning, and collective duration.

The "Third Space" is therefore not merely a distant island concept. It is a practical and ethical refusal to let human life be reduced to its most administratively convenient description.

### Final Reflection: The Restless Passage
As the sun sets over the Java Sea, the ritual is over, but the threshold lingers. The salt remains on the skin. The cadence of prayer remains in the chest. What has happened cannot be fully preserved in an archive, nor fully translated into policy.

To be human is not to belong to a single story, card, or category. It is to move, often uneasily, between several. Gili Iyang matters because it shows that this movement need not be understood as failure. It can also be a disciplined social practice, a ritualized way of surviving pressure without surrendering complexity.

Our task is not to eliminate structure or romanticize fluidity, for both are part of social life. It is to keep the threshold open: to preserve those spaces where communities can still negotiate change on terms richer than those offered by markets, metrics, or administrative forms.

The ritual continues not as a relic of what a community once was, but as an active resistance to premature closure. In that sense, the breath still matters more than the bit. It names what remains living precisely because it cannot be fully fixed.
